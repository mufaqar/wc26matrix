SCENE 06 How host city contracts work explained simply
Voiceover
To understand why FIFA could say no, you need to understand host city contracts. When a city bids to host World Cup matches, it signs a legally binding contract with FIFA. This contract — running hundreds of pages — specifies exactly how many matches the city will host, which stadiums will be used, and what the city must provide. The contract includes penalty clauses. If a city backs out, FIFA can demand compensation. And critically, the contract says match locations can only be changed by mutual agreement between FIFA and the host city. No president, no government, no third party can override it.

SCENE 07 Who actually controls World Cup match locations
Voiceover
So who actually decides where matches are played? The answer is FIFA's World Cup organizing committee, working in partnership with the host nation's local organizing committee. In the case of the 2026 World Cup — which is hosted by the United States, Canada, and Mexico — there's a tri-national organizing committee. Match locations are finalized years in advance through a bidding process. Cities submit bids, FIFA evaluates them on stadium quality, infrastructure, security, and accommodation. Once awarded, the locations are locked. No president, no prime minister, no king can unilaterally change them.

SCENE 08 The legal framework binding host cities to FIFA
Voiceover
The legal framework is ironclad. Host cities sign a "Host City Agreement" with FIFA that includes force majeure clauses, dispute resolution through the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS), and governing law usually under Swiss law where FIFA is headquartered. The contract also includes a "no government interference" clause — a direct response to decades of political meddling in football. This clause was strengthened after the 2014 Brazil World Cup and the 2018 Russia World Cup, where governments tried to influence operations. In legal terms, the host city agreement is one of the most airtight sports contracts in existence.

SCENE 09 What happens if a government refuses to cooperate
Voiceover
What would happen if the US government simply refused to cooperate? This is where things get serious. FIFA has a nuclear option — they can relocate the entire tournament. If a host government fails to meet its obligations, FIFA's Executive Committee can vote to move matches or even strip the hosting rights entirely. This has happened before. In 1986, Colombia was originally awarded the World Cup but couldn't meet FIFA's requirements and the tournament was moved to Mexico. In 2022, FIFA threatened to move the Qatar World Cup over human rights concerns before ultimately not doing so. The threat is real.
