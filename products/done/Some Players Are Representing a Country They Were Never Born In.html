Scene 1 — The Hook
Scene 01 Half the players at this World Cup could be playing for a different country. Here is why.
Voiceover

Look at any World Cup squad list this year and ask a simple question: how many of these players were actually born in the country they represent? The answer might surprise you. Dozens of players at the 2026 World Cup qualify through parents, grandparents, or even residency rules. Some have never lived in the country whose jersey they wear. This is the globalization of football — and it is transforming international football forever.

Scene 2 — FIFA Eligibility Rules Explained
Scene 02 Birthplace, parent, grandparent, residency requirements
Voiceover

FIFA outlines four clear pathways to represent a national team. One: born in that country. Two: a parent born there. Three: a grandparent born there. Four: two years of residency — extended to five for naturalized citizens with no ancestral link. That is it. A player can have zero connection to a country beyond a grandparent they never met, and legally wear that nation's shirt on the world's biggest stage.

Scene 3 — The One-Time Switch Rule
Scene 03 How players can change national teams once
Voiceover

In 2020, FIFA introduced a major rule change. Players who have played for one national team at youth level can now switch to another country — once — provided they had not played a competitive senior match for the first nation. It opened the floodgates. Dozens of players switched allegiances. Some had represented England at youth level and now play for Nigeria, Jamaica, Ghana. The one-time switch rule literally changed the map of world football.

Scene 5 — Why Players Switch
Scene 05 Better chance of playing, family heritage, multiple identities
Voiceover

Why do players switch? Three main reasons. Opportunity — a player might not make England's crowded squad but walks into Jamaica's starting eleven. Heritage — many feel a genuine connection to their family's country of origin. And identity — some players hold multiple nationalities and genuinely feel they belong to more than one nation. The decision is rarely simple. It involves family, career, pride, and sometimes, a difficult personal choice.

Scene 7 — Historical Context
Scene 07 How eligibility has evolved over World Cup history
Voiceover

This is not new. Alfredo Di Stefano played for Argentina, Colombia, and Spain. Ferenc Puskas represented Hungary and then Spain. In the early World Cups, players frequently switched nations — the rules were loose. But as the sport professionalized, eligibility tightened. The 1990s and 2000s saw stricter rules. And now, with the 2020 reforms, the pendulum has swung back. We are returning to an older, more fluid idea of national identity in football.

Scene 9 — FIFA's Rules Compared to Other Sports
Scene 09 Rugby, basketball, athletics
Voiceover

Every sport handles eligibility differently. Rugby has a five-year residency requirement, recently extended from three to protect the integrity of national teams. Basketball allows players to represent a country if they hold a passport, with no ancestry requirement. Athletics is strict — you must be a citizen and cannot switch without a three-year waiting period. FIFA sits somewhere in the middle — more flexible than athletics, stricter than basketball, evolving toward rugby's model.
